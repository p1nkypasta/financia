Unit 1 - The Nature and Importance of Economics
  • What is Economics?
    • Social Science of scarcity and choice
    • study of the way we make decisions about the use of scarce resources
  • Decision Making
    • Decisions made to try to satisfy all your wants with our limited resources.
    • If we face questions about what we need, what we want, and what we can afford to do, we are engaged in economic decision
      • Define the problem
      • Clarify goals & priorities
      • List the Possible Alternatives
      • Establish Criteria to use and judge the alternatives based on your goals/priorities
      • Evaluate each alternative
      • Make a Decision
      • Act on the Decision, and assess effectiveness (repeat if necessary)
  • Scarcity
    • A limited amount of resources available to produce a limited number of goods and services to meet relatively unlimited human wants
  • Opportunity Cost
    • Is the sum of all that is lost from taking one course of action over another
    • Always consider not just what we are gaining but also what we stand to lose.
    • That any time we use material goods to achieve one end, we have to do without something else
    • Law of increasing opportunity costs
    • As you produce anything, the opportunity cost to produce it will get bigger and bigger. Usually because resources needed to produce each resource are completely different.
  • Efficiency vs Effectiveness
    • Efficiency is using as few as possible resources to achieve the desired outcome.
    • Effectiveness is ensuring the best possible outcome considering the amount of resources.
    • Economic decisions MUST BE both effective AND efficient as that will be the maximum every time.
  • Production Possibility Curve
    • A curve illustrating PPC. One product is made on one axis, and the other product is made on the other axis. The curve represents the maximum amount of products manufactured considering it uses maximum efficiency and effectiveness.
    • Assumptions:
      • Only 2 commodities (goods or services) are produced
      • All resources are fully employed
  • Different Types of Economies
    • Traditional (small tribal) - Based on small economic units.
      • What → Based on resources available
      • How → Based on customs
      • Whom → Based on needs
    • Command (communist) - Everything is controlled and owned by the government. High degree of government regulation.
    • Market (capitalist) - Everything is privately owned. No degree of government involvement.
  • Circular Flow of Money
    • Macroeconomics
      • Studies the behavior of aggregate (total) economic variables such as income, employment, price level, etc. (large scale)
    • Microeconomics
      • Studies the behavior of individuals and groups of individuals, such as consumers, households, and businesses (small scale)
    • Traces the exchange of goods and services in the economy
    • two-way circular flow in the economy - of goods and services in one direction and money in the other direction - between: Consumers (Households), Businesses (Firms), Government, Other Countries (foreign)
  • GDP
    • Sectors of a mixed market economy:
      • Consumption (C) (household)
        • Spending by households on consumer goods and services
        • Primarily affected by consumer's disposable income (income remaining after taxes)
      • Gross investment (I) (business sector)
        • Creation of a new business or expansion of an existing one or the improvement in the production capability of a business
        • Basically just investing in something
      • Government (G)
        • Spending by all levels of government on goods and services; how much they can spend is affected by tax revenue
      • Foreign Sector (X - M)
        • Exports (add to the GDP as they enter the flow of money) (X)
        • Imports (subtract from the GDP) (M)
        • Adding together (aggregating) the four categories of spending gives us the GDP expenditure equation: (gross domestic product or total spending in the economy of the country)
          • GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
      • Excluded items from GDP:
        • Financial exchanges
          • Gifts of money between friends & family; bank deposits or stocks purchases
        • Second-hand purchases
  • Famous Economists
    • Adam Smith (lived 1700s)
      • The invisible hand theory (1759)
        • Refers to the invisible forces that move a free market economy
        • Through individual self-interest and freedom of production and consumption, the best interest of society, as a whole, are fulfilled.
        • Critics argue that the invisible hand does not always produce socially beneficial outcomes, and can encourage greed, negative externalities, inequalities, and other harms
        • Advocated for laissez-faire policies
    • John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946)
      • Keynesian economics
        • Argues that demand drives supply and that healthy economies spend or invest more than they save.
        • To create jobs and boost consumer buying power during a recession, Keynes held that governments should increase spending, even if it means going into debt.
        • Critics attack Keynesian economics for promoting deficit spending, stifling private investment, and causing inflation.
        • His idea that governments should play an active role in their countries' economies, instead of just letting the free market reign. Specifically, Keynes advocated federal spending to mitigate downturns in business cycles (came up with this after The Great Depression in '29).
  • Capitalism is the Crisis
    • Austerity - Refers to a set of economic policies that a government implements in order to control public sector debt. Governments put austerity measures in place when their public debt is so large that the risk of default or the inability to service the required payments on its obligations becomes a real possibility. Goal is to improve a government’s financial health.
    • Capitalism - System where a country’s trade and industries are controlled by private owners for profit.
    • Labour Union - An organized group of workers (often in the same trade) formed to protect their rights and freedoms.
    • Eco-socialism - Model that merges socialism with green ideals.
    • 2008 Collapse - 2008 stock market collapse. Stocks collapsed causing the greatest worldwide economic crisis since the Great Depression.
    • Greek crisis - Greece went into very large debt and was unable to pay it off, requiring bailout loans from other countries as they threatened to destroy the euro. Caused due to the government’s fiscal policies that included too much spending.
    • Sustainability - Policy that supports long-term economic growth without negatively affecting cultural, social, and environmental aspects of the community.
    • Democracy - A system of government where those in charge are elected representatives of the people.
