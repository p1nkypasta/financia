Unit 2 - Supply and Demand
  • Law of Supply
    • The quantity of a good/service that businesses are willing and able to provide within a range of prices that people would be willing to pay.
    • As the price of a good or service increases, the quantity that suppliers offer will increase.
  • Law of Demand
    • The quantity of a good/service that consumers are willing and able to buy at a particular price.
    • The higher the price, the lower the quantity demanded.
  • Market Structures
    • 4 Types
      • Pure Perfect Competition
        • Every company’s product is basically the same (look, feel, taste).
        • Product, price, and promotion are of little importance.
        • Compete on place (Distribution).
        • Examples: Tomatoes, Apples, Carrots, etc.
        • Almost anyone can enter the market with a little bit of cash and a small amount of supplies.
      • Monopolistic Competition
        • Many sellers competing in the same industry.
        • Products can be substituted.
        • Must use a marketing mix to differentiate (Price, Product, Promotion).
        • Examples: Shoes, clothing stores, restaurants, a mall.
        • Harder to get into, but not impossible. Usually requires a loan, a location, and some machinery.
      • Oligopoly
        • A few companies control the industry (2-7).
        • Do not want to compete on price, or else they lose revenue.
        • Compete on promotion, product, and place (Brand Loyalty).
        • Only a handful of major companies make up the industry.
        • Examples: Airlines, banks, auto industry, computer industry, oil, cell phones, internet.
        • Very difficult to get into. Would need a lot of money, infrastructure, patents, and factories.
      • Monopoly
        • Only one firm sells a product or service.
        • Often regulated by the government so they cannot raise prices to an insane level.
        • Examples: Bell Canada until deregulation, Postal Service, LCBO, Power Suppliers.
        • Almost impossible to get into due to cost, infrastructure, and time.
    • Elasticity
      • PED Numbers
        • If PED greater than 1, then demand is price elastic (sensitive to price changes).
        • If PED = 1, then demand is Unit Elastic.
        • If PED less than 1, then demand is price inelastic (not sensitive to price changes).
        • Price Elasticity of Demand = Percentage Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ Percentage Change in Price.
      • Factors that Affect Elasticity
        • Luxury vs. Necessity
          • Necessities tend to be inelastic.
          • Luxuries tend to be elastic.
        • Number of Substitutes
          • Many substitutes = elastic.
          • Few substitutes = inelastic.
        • Percentage of Household Budget
          • Small percentage of budget = inelastic.
          • Large percentage of budget = elastic.
        • Time Horizon
          • Short Run - Inelastic.
          • Long Run - Elastic.
      • Elastic vs. Inelastic
        • A product is considered elastic if the quantity demanded of the product changes drastically when its price increases or decreases.
        • A product is considered inelastic if the quantity demanded of the product changes very little when its price fluctuates.
    • Complement and Substitute Goods
      • Complement goods - goods that go along with the product.
      • Substitute goods - goods that replace the product.
    • Equilibrium, Shortage, Surplus
      • When supply matches demand.
      • Shortage - supply is less than demand.
      • Surplus - demand is less than supply.
    • Graphing Supply and Demand
      • Show changes (increase/decrease) to supply and/or demand.
      • Normal goods are products and services that see a rise in demand when incomes rise.
      • Inferior goods are products and services that see a decrease in demand as incomes rise.
